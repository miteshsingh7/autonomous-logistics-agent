## Slide 1 — Title
**Autonomous Logistics Rerouting: Open vs. Closed Model Readiness**
- One-line framing: "Can we replace a premium frontier model with a cheaper open
  model for self-healing rerouting — without taking on unacceptable risk?"

## Slide 2 — The business problem
- **Takeaway:** We want logistics that fix themselves — detect a delay, reroute
  automatically, before a human even notices.
- Depth: reactive ops → proactive, self-healing ops; agent detects telemetry
  anomaly, evaluates alternatives, dispatches a reroute autonomously.

## Slide 3 — Why the model choice matters ($$$ vs. risk)
- **Takeaway:** The frontier model is more reliable but costs more; the open model
  is far cheaper but we own the risk. This decision scales across millions of
  shipments.
- Depth: cost/token, self-hosting, data residency, SLA ownership.

## Slide 4 — How we evaluated (the key idea)
- **Takeaway:** We didn't just check "did it get the right answer" — we graded
  *how* it made every decision. A right answer via a reckless process is not safe
  to automate.
- Depth: **trajectory-based evaluation**; capture thoughts, tool calls,
  observations, errors, decision; score the whole sequence.

## Slide 5 — The agent (one diagram)
- **Takeaway:** A single agent design, run identically for both models — a fair
  head-to-head.
- Depth: LangGraph ReAct loop; tools = telemetry, alternatives, quote, dispatch;
  self-healing back-edge on tool failure.

## Slide 6 — Scoring rubric
- **Takeaway:** Four things that matter: does it use the tools correctly, is its
  reasoning sound, does it recover from failures, does it get the right outcome.
- Depth: tool accuracy (rules), reasoning (LLM-as-judge), recovery
  (fault-injection), success; weighted composite favouring process integrity.

## Slide 7 — Results (the money slide)
- **Takeaway:** Both models get the right outcome and both recover from failures.
  The difference is *reasoning discipline* — the open model sometimes acts before
  fully justifying the decision.
- Show the aggregate table:

| Model  | Tool | Reason | Recover | Success | Overall |
|--------|------|--------|---------|---------|---------|
| closed | 1.00 | 0.94   | 1.00    | 1.00    | 0.98    |
| open   | 1.00 | 0.74   | 1.00    | 1.00    | 0.91    |

## Slide 8 — What the gap means in practice
- **Takeaway:** On a $90k shipment, "rerouted correctly but without checking the
  quote" is a real risk. The gap shows up exactly where stakes are highest.
- Depth: open model skipped the firm-quote step on some runs; outcome-only evals
  would have missed this entirely.

## Slide 9 — Trade-off summary
- **Takeaway:** It's not "which is smarter" — it's "where does each belong."
- Depth table: reasoning reliability vs. cost / control / privacy / customisation /
  operational burden.

## Slide 10 — Recommendation
- **Takeaway:** **Tiered deployment.** Use the open model for routine, lower-value
  reroutes with guardrails; keep the frontier model (or a human) for high-value /
  high-severity decisions.
- Depth: mandatory quote-validation step; human-in-the-loop above a $ threshold;
  frontier model as arbiter in a multi-agent setup — captures most cost savings,
  protects the decisions that matter.

## Slide 11 — Rollout & risks
- **Takeaway:** Ship it behind guardrails, measure continuously, expand as trust
  grows.
- Depth: shadow-mode first (open model runs, doesn't dispatch); trajectory
  logging → process mining for regressions; judge-model bias mitigation; scale
  scenario suite.

## Slide 12 — Emerging techniques we'd invest in
- **Takeaway:** Multi-agent hybrids and self-healing loops let us use the cheap
  model safely.
- Depth: multi-agent orchestration, reflection loops, process mining on traces,
  specialised skill packs for logistics rules.
